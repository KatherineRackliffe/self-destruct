This Essay Will Self Destruct

A robot may not injure a human being or, through inaction, allow a human being to come to harm

Few things are as painful as getting a motor stuck in your hair. One day, as I was working on a robot for the First Tech Challenge robotics competition, my long blonde hair dangled too close to the motor, and as the motor spun, it caught my hair quickly and became a tangled mess. One of the boys on the team scoffed at me, muttering something about how girls shouldn’t work on robots. I tried to wear my hair in a ponytail after that.

A robot must obey the orders given to it by human beings except where such orders would conflict with the First Law.

In the play R.U.R., is it any coincidence that our sympathetic character voicing her concerns as to the sentience of the robots is a woman? The play references many revolutions at the time, and Helena Glory is a parody of many suffragette tropes at the time. “How do you know [the robots] have no soul?” Helena Glory asks. “Why do you manufacture female robots?” “There’s a certain demand for them, you see. Servants, saleswomen, stenographers. People are used to it.”
